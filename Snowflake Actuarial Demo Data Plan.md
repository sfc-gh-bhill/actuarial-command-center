## **Phase II: Generating Actuarially Realistic Synthetic Data**

Because authentic medical claims data is heavily restricted by the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) and other stringent privacy regulations, developing a scalable, shareable demonstration requires the use of synthetic data.7 However, basic randomization techniques—such as shuffling existing columns or using simple uniform distributions—fail to produce the complex longitudinal correlations, seasonal variations, and heavy-tailed financial distributions necessary for rigorous actuarial modeling.8 The synthetic data must intricately approximate the mathematical and statistical properties of real patient populations.9

### **Advanced Methodologies for Health Data Synthesis**

The generation of realistic synthetic claims data has evolved significantly beyond basic data masking. Modern generative artificial intelligence techniques, including Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) and Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), are increasingly utilized to create synthetic medical claims.7 These deep learning models are trained on existing, highly secure datasets to learn the underlying joint distributions of patient demographics, diagnoses, procedural interventions, and financial outcomes.7 For instance, specialized adapted ADS-GAN models can produce large-scale, highly realistic datasets that preserve the covariate distribution and treatment assignment mechanisms found in real-world cohorts, achieving exceptionally low identifiability metrics to ensure total patient privacy.10

Alternatively, agent-based modeling approaches, such as the open-source Synthea framework, generate synthetic patient records by simulating the longitudinal medical history of individuals from birth to death.12 These simulated patients progress through modular representations of various diseases, interacting with the synthetic healthcare system to generate incredibly realistic, chronological encounter and claims data that maintain strict temporal ordering.12

### **Enforcing Actuarial Probability Distributions**

For the synthetic data to withstand the scrutiny of a Chief Actuary, the generated financial variables—specifically the Claim Allowed Amount and the Plan Paid Amount—must follow specific, highly skewed statistical distributions recognized in casualty and health actuarial science. Healthcare claim costs are strictly non-negative; they have a hard lower bound of zero but range widely on the upper end due to catastrophic health events.16 Consequently, normal (Gaussian) distributions are entirely inadequate for modeling medical costs.17

Actuarial research overwhelmingly dictates that claim severity is best modeled using the Gamma distribution or the Lognormal distribution.16 The generation models driving the Snowflake demonstration must be calibrated to reflect these specific mathematical realities:

1. **The Gamma Distribution for Outpatient Severity:** The Gamma distribution is widely favored for modeling standard outpatient, pharmacy, and professional claim severity due to its superior predictive accuracy, stability, and efficiency.16 The probability density function of the Gamma distribution accommodates the continuous, positively skewed nature of routine medical billing, where the vast majority of claims cluster around lower dollar amounts, but a distinct right tail exists for more complex office procedures.16  
2. **The Lognormal Distribution for Inpatient Severity:** For high-cost, severe claims—such as MS-DRG based inpatient hospital stays and complex surgical interventions—the Lognormal distribution is the actuarial standard. The Lognormal model effectively captures the extreme, explosive variance associated with catastrophic medical events, where the variance is often proportional to the mean squared.17 If a random variable follows a lognormal distribution, its natural logarithm yields a normal distribution.18 This heavy-tailed characteristic ensures that the synthetic dataset includes the rare but financially devastating million-dollar claims that drive stop-loss reinsurance modeling.  
3. **Claim Frequency:** The occurrence of claim events (the number of discrete claims per member per month) must be modeled independently from severity, typically utilizing a Poisson or Negative Binomial distribution to represent the discrete count of events over a specific exposure period.18

If the synthetic data generation pipeline inadvertently defaults to a normal distribution for claim costs, the resulting Health Plan Co.l Loss Ratio (MLR) and Margin-at-risk simulations will lack the intrinsic volatility and tail-risk that actuaries are specifically trained to manage and mitigate, immediately rendering the demonstration unrealistic.

### **Incurred But Not Reported (IBNR) Reserves**

A fundamental complexity in healthcare economics is the operational delay between the date a medical service is rendered to a patient and the date the corresponding claim is fully adjudicated and paid by the health plan.20 Providers submit claims at highly variable speeds; a sophisticated urban hospital system utilizing electronic data interchange (EDI) may submit claims within days, whereas a rural facility or specialized behavioral health provider may batch and submit claims weeks or months later.27 Furthermore, prior authorization disputes and coding edits can introduce significant delays into the adjudication cycle.27

Because of this inherent "claims lag," health plans carry a massive, hidden liability for services that have already occurred but are not yet reflected in the paid claims database.27 Actuaries must utilize predictive modeling to estimate this liability, known as the Incurred But Not Reported (IBNR) reserve.27 Accurate IBNR estimation is critical; auditors require exhaustive documentation of IBNR methodologies, and inaccurate reserving directly impacts corporate earnings volatility.27

To estimate the "Ultimate Incurred Claims," actuaries traditionally construct a claims lag grid, commonly referred to as a run-off triangle.4 This two-dimensional matrix maps the historical detail of actual claim amounts, cross-tabulating the month the claim was incurred (the rows) against the subsequent months of development until the claim was paid (the columns).4

By analyzing historical run-off patterns, actuaries calculate age-to-age development factors (often called link ratios) to derive a cumulative "Completion Factor" for each incurral month.29 The completion factor represents the percentage of the ultimate claim liability that is assumed to be paid by a specific point in time.29

The following table illustrates a simplified derivation of actuarial completion factors for an IBNR calculation model:

| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Month 0 (Current) | $30,000 | 1.67 | 29.1% | $103,092 |
| Month 1 | $55,000 | 1.81 | 52.7% | $104,364 |
| Month 2 | $75,000 | 1.34 | 70.8% | $105,932 |
| Month 3 | $84,000 | 1.13 | 80.0% | $105,000 |
| Month 4 (Ultimate) | $105,000 | 1.00 | 100.0% | $105,000 |

Note: Data architecture based on standard actuarial methodology derivations.27

For example, if the historical run-off triangle indicates that only 30% of a given month's claims are historically paid within that same initial month (a completion factor of 0.30), and the current month shows $30,000 in actual paid claims, the actuary estimates the total ultimate incurred claims for that month to be $100,000 ($30,000 / 0.30).27 The difference between the estimated ultimate ($100,000) and the paid-to-date ($30,000) yields an IBNR reserve requirement of $70,000 for that specific cohort.27

However, traditional multiplicative chain-ladder models applied to real data often exhibit an upward bias and can become highly unstable when encountering sparse data or zero-claim development periods.30 To mitigate this, actuaries frequently blend methodologies, utilizing the Bornhuetter-Ferguson method or additive regression models, which heavily weight an initial expectation of the ultimate loss ratio for recent, highly immature months where the completion factor is precariously low.30

### **CMS-HCC Risk Adjustment and Revenue Exposure**

Beyond direct claims costs, a health plan's revenue exposure is inextricably linked to the underlying morbidity and clinical acuity of its enrolled population.32 In Health Plan Co.re Advantage, Affordable Care Act (ACA) commercial markets, and many state Health Plan Co.id managed care programs, revenue is modulated by risk adjustment (RA) models.33 The demonstration must accurately account for Risk Adjustment Factor (RAF) scores to demonstrate revenue protection capabilities.1

The primary mechanism for this is the Centers for Health Plan Co.re & Health Plan Co.id Services Hierarchical Condition Category (CMS-HCC) model.32 The CMS-HCC model adjusts capitated payments from the federal government to Health Plan Co.re Advantage Organizations (MAOs) based on the expected cost to care for beneficiaries, determined by their specific demographic profiles and documented chronic medical conditions.34

The calculation of these risk scores is highly complex and subject to intense regulatory revision. For example, in the transition to the updated v28 model for calendar year 2024, CMS instituted profound structural changes to the HCC model.32 To curtail aggressive coding practices and correct for payment inaccuracies, CMS applied constraint logic to several high-prevalence disease categories.32 Specifically, the revised model constrains the values of all diabetes-related HCCs (HCCs 36, 37, and 38\) to apply equal coefficients, and similarly constrains all congestive heart failure (CHF) HCCs (HCCs 224, 225, and 226).32 Furthermore, specific categories such as protein-calorie malnutrition and angina pectoris were removed from the model entirely.32

For an actuarial demonstration to be deemed realistic, the back-end logic modeling RAF erosion risk and revenue sensitivity must programmatically reflect these specific v28 coefficient constraints.1 A model that assumes linear, unconstrained revenue growth based on outdated v24 diabetes coding intensity will be immediately recognized as flawed by a practicing health actuary.

## **Phase IV: Designing the Truth Layer with Snowflake Semantic Views**

A critical point of failure in modern enterprise AI implementations is the direct exposure of Large Language Models to highly normalized, complex physical database schemas. When an AI agent attempts to generate SQL directly against raw tables to answer a financial question, it inevitably makes incorrect assumptions regarding table joins, filter conditions, and aggregation logic, leading to inconsistent metric definitions across different queries. To definitively solve the "CFO-Level Bridge," the demonstration must prove unequivocally that the visual Streamlit dashboard and the conversational AI agent are extracting their answers from the exact same, mathematically governed truth.1

Snowflake Semantic Views resolve this structural vulnerability by allowing data architects to store semantic business concepts directly as schema-level objects within the database infrastructure.35 By adding explicit business meaning to physical data, the semantic view provides a consistent, authoritative interface for both Business Intelligence (BI) tools and Cortex Analyst.35 Crucially, because Semantic Views are native database objects, they inherently integrate with Snowflake's Role-Based Access Control (RBAC).36 This guarantees that if row-level security masking policies dictate that a VP of Product for ACA cannot view Health Plan Co.id financial margins, that user cannot circumvent the restriction by querying the AI agent; the agent is bound by the identical security context as a standard SQL query.1

### **Semantic View Architecture for Health Actuarial Data**

Instead of embedding fragile, hardcoded MLR and IBNR calculation logic directly into the Streamlit application's Python code or relying on an LLM to intuit the math, the logic is centrally, permanently defined using the CREATE SEMANTIC VIEW Data Definition Language (DDL) command.1

A well-architected semantic view for an actuarial data model consists of five highly structured components:

1. **Logical Tables:** The foundation of the view, mapping directly to physical base tables (e.g., mapping a logical claims entity to the physical DEMO\_DB.GOLD.SYNTH\_MEDICAL\_CLAIMS table) and identifying primary keys to prevent Cartesian explosions.35  
2. **Relationships:** Explicitly defining the cardinality and join paths between logical tables. In this model, the claims table is joined to the enrollment table via the shared MEMBER\_ID key.37  
3. **Facts:** The granular, row-level numerical attributes that represent specific business events or financial transactions. For the healthcare model, critical facts include the raw claim paid amounts (CDLMC125), billed charges, and the specific dates of service.35 Unlike dimensions, facts are not grouped by the query engine.38  
4. **Dimensions:** The categorical attributes utilized for filtering, slicing, and grouping the analytical queries. Crucial dimensions in the actuarial model include the Line of Business (e.g., ACA, Health Plan Co.re Advantage, Health Plan Co.id), Geographic Location (e.g., Texas vs. Minnesota), Service Category (e.g., Inpatient, Outpatient, Behavioral Health), and Provider NPI.1 Synonyms can be attached to dimensions (e.g., defining 'LOB' as a synonym for 'Product') to vastly improve the natural language recognition of Cortex Analyst.37  
5. **Metrics:** The quantifiable, aggregate measures calculating true business performance.35 Metrics transform raw facts into actionable insights through complex aggregations (SUM, AVG, COUNT).35 The semantic view defines the absolute, unalterable logic for the Health Plan Co.l Loss Ratio, Margin-at-risk, and Per Member Per Month (PMPM) costs within this section.1

The following SQL DDL illustrates the construction of the governing actuarial semantic view:

SQL

CREATE OR REPLACE SEMANTIC VIEW actuarial\_financial\_truth  
TABLES (  
  claims AS DEMO\_DB.GOLD.SYNTH\_MEDICAL\_CLAIMS PRIMARY KEY (claim\_id),  
  enrollment AS DEMO\_DB.GOLD.SYNTH\_MEMBER\_ELIGIBILITY PRIMARY KEY (member\_id)  
)  
RELATIONSHIPS (  
  claims (member\_id) REFERENCES enrollment  
)  
FACTS (  
  claims.paid\_amount AS plan\_paid\_amt,  
  claims.incurred\_date AS date\_of\_service,  
  enrollment.premium\_revenue AS capitation\_revenue  
)  
DIMENSIONS (  
  enrollment.line\_of\_business AS lob WITH SYNONYMS \= ('market', 'product', 'segment'),  
  claims.service\_category AS service\_type WITH SYNONYMS \= ('claim type', 'service category'),  
  claims.provider\_state AS state  
)  
METRICS (  
  claims.total\_medical\_expense AS SUM(plan\_paid\_amt) COMMENT \= 'Total aggregate claim liability',  
  enrollment.total\_premium AS SUM(capitation\_revenue) COMMENT \= 'Total aggregate capitation revenue',  
  claims.medical\_loss\_ratio AS SUM(plan\_paid\_amt) / NULLIF(SUM(capitation\_revenue), 0) COMMENT \= 'Core MLR calculation tracking regulatory FP\&A standards'  
);

By defining the MLR centrally as a metric within the semantic view, when a CAO asks the Snowflake Intelligence Cortex Agent, "What is our current MLR for the ACA market in Texas?", the agent does not attempt to write a complex join query from scratch. Instead, it generates a highly deterministic query: SELECT \* FROM SEMANTIC\_VIEW(actuarial\_financial\_truth METRICS(medical\_loss\_ratio) DIMENSIONS(lob, state)).1 This architectural decision guarantees that the AI returns exact, reconcilable financial outputs that tie flawlessly to the enterprise FP\&A systems.

### **The Complex Mechanics of Hospital Contracting**

Hospital pricing dynamics and unit cost inflation are fundamentally dictated by the structure of payer-provider contracts. Hospitals and large health systems operating in consolidated geographic markets often possess immense market power, allowing them to negotiate highly favorable reimbursement terms from insurers.48

These contracts are riddled with complex provisions designed to maintain provider revenue margins. Common anticompetitive clauses that actuaries must model include:

* **Most Favored Nation (MFN) Clauses:** A guarantee that an insurer receives terms from a hospital that are at least as favorable as those provided to any competing payer, effectively setting a market price floor.48  
* **All-or-Nothing Clauses:** A requirement that a health plan must contract with every single facility within a dominant health system if they wish to include any of the system's "must-have" flagship hospitals in their network.48  
* **Anti-Tiering / Anti-Steering Clauses:** Contractual mandates that prohibit insurers from utilizing financial incentives (such as lower copays) to steer patients away from expensive health system facilities toward lower-cost, independent competitors.48

Furthermore, general macroeconomic inflation has severely impacted hospital operating margins. Labor costs, exacerbated by severe nursing shortages, now account for a staggering 56% of total hospital expenses.50 To combat this, hospitals aggressively trigger Price Adjustment Clauses in their payer contracts, demanding sweeping rate increases to offset the escalating cost of raw materials, labor, and technology.51

For major inpatient care, the actual financial reimbursement is predominantly governed by the Health Plan Co.re Severity Diagnosis-Related Group (MS-DRG) methodology.52 Under the DRG system, the final reimbursement amount paid to the hospital is calculated by multiplying the relative weight of the assigned MS-DRG code (which mathematically reflects the specific patient's severity of illness and expected resource consumption) by a highly negotiated, hospital-specific base rate.53 A minor fluctuation in this negotiated base rate, or an error in DRG coding (such as failing to code complications for an MS-DRG 481 femur fracture), drastically alters the final payment trajectory, swinging hospital revenue and payer liability by thousands of dollars per encounter.55

When the user moves the slider, the architecture seamlessly executes a sophisticated chain of events:

1. **Dynamic Parameter Passing:** The Streamlit front-end captures the new baseline input parameter (-2.00%) and passes it instantly as an argument to a Snowflake Python Stored Procedure residing in the Data Cloud.1  
2. **Actuarial Logic Execution:** The Python Stored Procedure, utilizing libraries analogous to drgpy for rapid MS-DRG classification and manipulation 52, instantly recalculates the entire contract repricing simulation across millions of rows of synthetic data.1 It identifies all MS-DRG inpatient claims and high-cost behavioral health claims associated with the targeted Texas provider network.  
3. **Algorithmic Repricing:** The script applies the 200 basis point reduction to the existing base rates. It recalculates the CDLMC131 Allowed Amounts for every affected claim line, factors in the invariant member cost-sharing elements, and aggregates the newly projected total medical expense.3  
4. **Real-Time Margin Update:** The heavily revised financial metrics are passed back from Snowflake to the UI, instantly updating the Year-End Margin forecast visualization and adjusting the statistical confidence intervals rendered on the screen.1

This rapid, highly scalable scenario stress-testing proves to the CAO that the Snowflake platform can perform volume-heavy, highly complex actuarial computations—simulating the financial ripple effects of complex contract negotiations across massive claims datasets—seamlessly and instantaneously, without resorting to fragile Excel workbooks or batch processing delays.

## **Phase VII: The Intelligence Agent and Unstructured Data Integration**

The final, capstone component of the demonstration addresses the crucial "CFO-Level Bridge" by proving the absolute efficacy and reliability of Snowflake Intelligence.1 Real-world actuarial work is not strictly confined to analyzing structured SQL tables; it relies heavily on the exhaustive interpretation of unstructured qualitative data, including dense regulatory guidelines, complex provider contracts, and hundreds of pages of actuarial memorandums detailing utilization trends.56

### **Cortex Search Indexing of Contractual Documents**

To facilitate this unstructured analysis, the architecture utilizes Snowflake Cortex Search to embed, index, and retrieve complex policy documents.1 During the data engineering pipeline phase, dozens of sample PDF documents representing hospital provider contracts, termination clauses, and lengthy rate schedule amendments are loaded securely into an internal Snowflake stage.58

Using the powerful AI\_PARSE\_DOCUMENT function, the dense legal text is systematically extracted and formatted as JSON strings.58 The architecture then processes this raw text, breaking the massive contracts into specific text fragments or "chunks" of a specific size to facilitate optimal search and retrieval.58 These chunks are then vectorized and indexed by the Cortex Search Service, which leverages an advanced E5 embedding model to deeply understand the complex semantic relationships buried within the legal terminology.59 This configuration enables an exceptionally fast hybrid search mechanism, combining both semantic intent and precise keyword matching.59

### **The Multi-Turn Agentic Workflow**

Inside the Streamlit application, the user opens the Snowflake Intelligence chat panel and prompts the Cortex Agent with a multifaceted business question: *"Why is our Texas margin missing the target, and exactly what is our exposure?"*.1

The architecture of the Cortex Agent processes this complex query using a highly orchestrated set of specialized tools, ensuring the answer is both mathematically precise and legally contextualized:

1. **Structured Data Retrieval:** The Agent first recognizes the need for numerical financial variance. It triggers a tool to query the governed actuarial\_financial\_truth Semantic View.1 Because the view defines the exact parameters of the MLR, the Agent extracts the precise, deterministic dollar amount of the margin miss that was driven by the 8% behavioral health unit cost spike.1  
2. **Unstructured Contextual Retrieval:** Simultaneously, the Agent triggers Cortex Search to scan the indexed corpus of unstructured provider contracts.1 It rapidly retrieves the specific Texas hospital system contract PDF. Utilizing Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), it locates the specific inflation-adjusted rate increase clause within the text, reading the legal document to determine the exact contractual date the rate increase legally took effect.1  
3. **Synthesized Output:** The Agent intelligently combines the deterministic financial figures extracted from the Semantic View with the qualitative contractual context derived from Cortex Search.1 It delivers a single, verified, and legally contextualized natural language answer to the user.1

This sophisticated workflow definitively eliminates the CAO's "black box" concern. Because the financial data is strictly constrained by the mathematically governed Semantic View, and the contractual data is explicitly grounded via Cortex Search RAG on verified PDFs, the executive can implicitly trust that the AI agent is not fabricating financial variance or hallucinating legal terminology.

## **Strategic Conclusion**

Building a compelling, enterprise-grade actuarial demonstration requires a delicate equilibrium between sophisticated, consumer-grade user experience and uncompromising mathematical governance. By leveraging the comprehensive capabilities of the Snowflake AI Data Cloud, data architects can synthesize millions of rows of strictly formatted APCD-CDL claims data using native, privacy-preserving generative functions. By enforcing Gamma and Lognormal statistical parameters during this generation, the underlying data flawlessly reflects the highly skewed, heavy-tailed realities of true health economics.

The instantiation of Snowflake Semantic Views acts as the critical, load-bearing linchpin in this architecture. By transforming raw, physical tables into a governed, queryable Truth Layer, the architecture defines crucial MLR, IBNR, and risk score metrics identically for both traditional BI reporting and advanced AI agents. When this governed foundation is combined with Streamlit's interactive parameter passing for rapid MS-DRG contract repricing simulations, and Cortex's unparalleled ability to blend time-series anomaly detection with the deep semantic analysis of unstructured provider contracts, the resulting asset completely transcends the limitations of a standard dashboard. It manifests as a comprehensive, highly defensible actuarial engine, capable of definitively demonstrating to elite finance leaders exactly how complex, multi-million-dollar margin-at-risk exposures can be proactively detected, rigorously modeled, and effectively mitigated in real-time.
